# Work-to-Rule

The most instructive strike in labor history doesn't involve walking out. In a work-to-rule action, employees do exactly what the manual says — every procedure, every checklist, every sign-off in sequence — and the institution seizes up. Trains run hours late. Permit offices back up for months. The French call it *grève du zèle*: the zeal strike. Its weapon is obedience, and it works everywhere it's tried, which should disturb anyone who believes organizations run on their written rules, because it proves they don't. The official procedure, executed faithfully, is a denial-of-service attack on the institution it claims to describe. And notice what that rulebook actually is: the residue of a hundred-year project to compile human judgment into executable procedure. Taylor with his stopwatch, the aviation checklist, the SOP binder, the approval chain — management has been doing program synthesis by hand since 1911, writing symbolic programs and running them on human processors. We had symbolic AI half a century before we had computers. It is not a coincidence that the same man wrote the foundational text on how organizations decide and co-authored the first artificial intelligence program.<span class="marginnote">Herbert Simon published <span class="inline-code">Administrative Behavior</span> in 1947 and co-wrote the Logic Theorist, arguably the first AI program, in 1956. The Turing Award (1975) and the Nobel in economics (1978) went to the same body of work.</span> Herbert Simon had one career because it was one project.

And it failed the way symbolic AI always fails. The expert systems of the 1980s were built by interviewing specialists and hand-coding their knowledge into rules: magnificent in the demo, then dead by a thousand exceptions, because the world leaks and symbols rot while reality moves. Every corporate rulebook dies the same death — I once built [a procurement system with over a thousand configuration parameters](/ai/2025/07/15/Building-on-Quicksand-The-Reality-of-Production-AI-Systems.html), each one true on the day we wrote it — and every institution survives its dead rulebook the same way: people patch it at runtime. The scheduler who knows who's owed the good shift. The clerk who knows what step fourteen actually means in October. Which means organizations already run the architecture [Chollet says AI is converging toward](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.04604) — intuition-guided symbolic modeling — and have for a century, with humans as the intuition layer wedged into every gap the symbols miss. What changed is not the architecture. What changed is that intuition became manufacturable, and with it something bureaucracy never had: rules that can be re-synthesized from observed reality instead of hand-written by whoever held the pen in 1994, with the exceptions folded back in as they occur. The model alone won't get you there; pure intuition fails the institution in the mirror-image way, absorbing all the mess and producing nothing an auditor can sign. Neural on the way in, symbolic on the way out is not a research aesthetic. Inside an institution it is the only architecture that survives both contact with reality and contact with a lawyer.

So the asset of the next decade isn't the model, which is being commoditized by the quarter, and it isn't the old rulebook, which was a fossil the day it was printed. It's the living program: rules synthesized from a real institution, still warm, still checkable by the people who have to answer for them. But extraction has an observer effect, and here the project should make you nervous. Not everything unwritten is a documentation failure. A favor written down becomes a debt. Discretion written down becomes policy, then liability. The fairness ledger a good manager keeps in her head works *because* it is deniable — James Scott's whole point about work-to-rule was that formal order is parasitic on an informal order it cannot acknowledge without destroying. Write everything down and you get high modernism with better tooling. The gap between the rules and the institution was never empty space; it's where mercy, favors, and every unrecorded negotiation lived. The zeal strike worked because the rules were wrong about the institution. For the first time, we can make them right — and almost nobody is asking what we lose when obeying the letter stops being a threat.
