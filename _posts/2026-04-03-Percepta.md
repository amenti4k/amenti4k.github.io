*Healthcare lens throughout, but most of this generalizes.*

## Three premises I'm treating as given

- Models will keep getting better and the improvement is compounding. The thing that's hard to internalize: plan for a world where the model is meaningfully better in 12 months and dramatically better in 24. <span class="marginnote">Scaling laws have delivered since 2012, and now models are being used to improve the training of the next generation, so the curve feeds on itself. METR task duration doubles every 4 months.</span>

- People and organizations will defer more and more decisions to models. The trend is one-directional. As models get more capable the surface area of delegation expands. <span class="marginnote">Already happening in consumer (search, writing, coding), beginning in enterprise. The bottleneck shifts from "can the model do this" to "can the organization hand this off." That handoff is the hard part.</span>

- Two problems stay hard and get harder as models improve. These are the durable seams:

- <span class="highlight-text">Observability.</span> When an agent takes an action you need to know what it did, why, and whether the outcome was correct. UI/UX for making agent behavior legible to humans. Plotting multi-agent trajectories when the state space explodes. This problem gets worse with capability. More powerful models take more actions across more domains, which means more to watch. <span class="marginnote">The verification inversion: checking the work gets harder as the worker gets smarter.</span>

- <span class="highlight-text">Verifiability conversion.</span> Most of what organizations do is not structured in a way an AI can execute and a human can verify the result. The work is messy, implicit, embedded in human judgment and social context. The core question: how do you take a process that currently lives in someone's head (or in the space between three people's heads) and convert it into something where you can check whether the AI did it right? <span class="marginnote">How do you get the human in the loop to actually verify, not rubber-stamp, but meaningfully confirm? I think this is the binding constraint right now. The model can already do enormous amounts of work. Whether anyone can confirm it was done correctly is what's actually gating deployment.</span>

- If these premises hold, <span class="highlight-text">the company that matters most is the one that solves the third problem.</span> Not the best model (those commoditize) and not the best vertical tool (delegation moves faster than any single tool). The one that can make organizations legible and their processes verifiable. <span class="marginnote">I could be wrong about this being the binding constraint. Maybe the real bottleneck is organizational identity, political resistance, or just that institutions move on generational timescales regardless of what's technically possible. But I'm betting it's verifiability.</span>

## Percepta is distinct from the labs, and that's the point

- The labs train intelligence centrally. Percepta deploys it locally, where institutional context lives. You can't manage a hospital's billing exceptions from the cloud. The context is too local, too much gets generated in real time by the people in the room. <span class="highlight-text">Intelligence has to come to where the context is.</span>

- What a lab has not done so far: go into a specific hospital, spend months understanding its 42-step billing process, map which steps are load-bearing, convert them into verifiable tasks, and build the observability layer around them. <span class="marginnote">Bob McGrew (OpenAI's former head of research) says each enterprise integration problem is "too small" for a frontier lab to solve. A lab looks at an enterprise problem and thinks about what model to train. The deployment question is different: which rooms do you sit in, which people do you talk to, what does the work actually look like up close.</span>

- The open question is whether this is structural or temporal. Labs are already hiring forward-deployed teams. Anthropic is building its own enterprise deployment capability. If it becomes lucrative enough, do they build the rest? <span class="marginnote">Or is there something about the lab business model (the researcher culture, the scale requirements, the ambition level) that makes granular institutional work permanently unattractive? I'm betting on the latter but I want to be honest that it's a bet.</span>

- Percepta is closer to Anthropic's edge network than an API customer. The structural logic: Anthropic sells tokens, Percepta minimizes token burn per outcome. Structurally complementary. <span class="marginnote">Percepta generates domain-specific training data from trusted deployments that Anthropic can't get from API usage logs. It comes from inside institutions, with consent, in structured form. That data may be worth more to Anthropic than the token revenue.</span>

- One version of where this goes looks like the OpenAI/Thrive deal. Anthropic researchers embedded in healthcare deployments, producing training data they can't get elsewhere. Percepta getting capabilities nobody else has. <span class="marginnote">Both locked in because both sides produce something the other needs.</span>

- Alma matters here. Deploying inside systems GC actually owns means you observe context at the source instead of negotiating for access on a consulting contract. Hard to overstate how different that is operationally.

## Percepta is not a holding company

- The holdcos buy businesses first, then try to transform them. They're on a fund clock. They have to pick targets before they deeply understand what's transformable. <span class="marginnote">LLMH, Thrive, Sequence. The operational improvements are real. Tasks that took 10 hours now take 1, margins genuinely expand. The question they haven't answered: does the market eventually reclassify an AI-transformed services company, or does the services label stick?</span>

- Percepta does it in reverse. Deploy into organizations, build the transformation toolkit, map what's actually possible, then decide what to buy. <span class="highlight-text">Transform first, own what you've converted.</span> <span class="marginnote">Whether having the deepest understanding of a business makes you the natural owner or just the most informed advisor is an open question, but at minimum it means you have information nobody else has at the point of acquisition.</span>

- I keep coming back to this optionality: Percepta can become a holdco in the future, but it gets to build the engine meticulously first. Try things out, learn what works, understand what's actually transformable, before buying anything. <span class="marginnote">The holdcos are doing the reverse and are forced to be rushed where Percepta can be methodical.</span>

- GC's permanent capital structure makes this possible. No fund timeline, no LP markup pressure. <span class="marginnote">GC bought a hospital. No VC fund does that. Partnering with major acquirers to deploy into enterprises going private removes quarterly earnings pressure entirely.</span>

- The distinction between automation and amplification matters here. Rollups automate to cut costs, which cannibalizes the labor revenue they bought. Percepta should be doing something different. Not making the hospital cheaper but making it capable of things that weren't possible before at any price. <span class="highlight-text">An exoskeleton rather than a replacement.</span>

## Percepta is not a typical AI startup

- Most AI startups converge on the same idea: build a vertical tool and compete for enterprise attention. Meanwhile the labs eat the bigger horizontal problems from above and capabilities double on 12-18 month cycles. Any startup building a narrow tool is racing against the model itself making that tool redundant.

- But there is a massive and growing delta between what the latest models can do and how organizations actually use them. More capability producing more failures, which is counterintuitive until you think about it. <span class="highlight-text">The intelligence keeps expanding and organizations aren't absorbing it.</span> <span class="marginnote">42% of enterprise AI initiatives discontinued in 2024, up from 17% the year before. You can see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics.</span>

- The question that separates real from performative in this space: can you generate $1 of real EBITDA for your customers? A lot of companies show compelling demos and pitch decks. Fewer can point to concrete economic value that materialized because of what they built. <span class="marginnote">The ones that can seem to have something in common: they went into the institution and did the work. Not from the screen. Sitting with operators, earning trust, watching people mark up printouts with highlighters. Whether that pattern holds universally or just in the verticals I've been looking at, I'm less sure.</span>

## The "every company becomes an API" thesis is insufficient

- One framing of the future: capabilities are here, just automate everything. That framing captures something real. But it's incomplete. You need to actually deploy these things into organizations that were not designed for them. <span class="marginnote">How much depth do you need for each domain? Is 80% from outside enough for most use cases, or does the last 20% where institutional reality lives matter disproportionately? I think it's the latter in healthcare and government. Less sure about other verticals.</span>

- What FDE teams are really doing is <span class="highlight-text">making organizations machine-readable.</span> Workflows made explicit, decision criteria and exception paths documented. Converting companies from human-social structures into something AI can execute against. The hospital that's been made legible can participate in whatever comes next. The one that hasn't is invisible to it.

- But this conversion can't happen from outside. A hospital's 42-step billing process wasn't designed by an idiot. It's organizational scar tissue that encodes real institutional knowledge. <span class="highlight-text">The process IS the documentation.</span> You can't automate it from outside. <span class="marginnote">Built after something went wrong, probably multiple times. Each step is a lesson about a failure mode nobody documented any other way.</span>

- You have to understand why each step exists, which are load-bearing and which are vestigial, and then convert the load-bearing ones into forms an AI can execute and a human can verify. <span class="marginnote">This is why the product can't be a SaaS dashboard you sell to the CTO. The work is ethnographic before it's technical.</span>

## The product is amorphous on purpose

- The "product" at Percepta is not a dashboard or an API or a SaaS tool. It's what lets smart, AI-pilled people usher intelligence into critical industries. Mosaic, Lattice, whatever comes next. The tooling exists to make those people hyper-capable of doing the actual work. <span class="marginnote">I haven't seen or used Mosaic, Lattice, or any of Percepta's internal tooling. What follows is what I think winning looks like based on the worldview above. My vision of what the product should be, not a description of what it is.</span>

- What the actual work is: deep understanding of how people work and how processes function inside organizations. Not surface-level process mapping. The kind you can only get by being embedded for months, earning trust, learning why things are the way they are.

- The tooling should give Percepta's people the ability to classify what's verifiable and what isn't across the full landscape of tasks inside an organization. For tasks that are already verifiable: deploy AI, monitor, iterate. For tasks that aren't verifiable: this is where the real work lives. Breaking down non-verifiable things into chunks of verifiable tasks. <span class="marginnote">Taking a messy, judgment-heavy, socially-embedded process and converting it into discrete steps an AI can execute and a human can meaningfully confirm. That decomposition is Percepta's core intellectual work.</span>

- <span class="marginnote">Whether that decomposition can be systematized into a platform or is inherently bespoke, requiring senior judgment every time, determines whether Percepta scales or stays boutique.</span> There's a real question about whether the decomposition skill transfers across verticals. The industry has mostly converged on single-vertical integration (one company for legal, one for healthcare, one for insurance). That assumes what you learn making a hospital legible doesn't help you make a government agency legible. I'm not sure that's right. <span class="marginnote">The specific domain knowledge doesn't transfer, but the methodology might: decomposing processes into verifiable tasks, building golden datasets of what "good" looks like, running reinforcement loops where operators improve the AI as they go. If that compounds across domains, Percepta's multi-vertical approach is an advantage. If it doesn't, spreading across verticals is dilution. The answer probably depends on whether Mosaic captures the methodology or just the domain-specific outputs.</span>

- The observability layer gets harder, not easier. Agent behavior needs to be legible to humans in real time. When multiple agents interact across a healthcare system, the state space explodes. You need tools to follow what happened, why, and whether the outcome was right. <span class="marginnote">Human-in-the-loop is an architectural decision, not an afterthought. Design the entire system so human verification is meaningful and efficient, not a rubber stamp bolted on at the end.</span>

- Traditional view of the FDE: go implement software at the client. Better view: Mosaic and whatever other tools Percepta creates are abstractions weaponized by FDEs. The goal is to get systems, processes, interactions, everything a company does operationally, into something legible by the model and verifiable by the model.

- Deployment complexity gets worse with better models. <span class="highlight-text">Context velocity</span> (how fast the AI system learns from resolved exceptions) is nearly asymptotic. <span class="highlight-text">Meaning velocity</span> (how fast humans metabolize that into trust) is not. Better models speed up context velocity without touching meaning velocity. The gap between what AI can do and what institutions will allow widens with each capability jump. <span class="marginnote">Approval chains, sign-offs, processes with legally fixed minimum durations. This is why the FDE role evolves but doesn't disappear. From deploying AI to governing it to evolving how governance itself works.</span>

- <span class="marginnote">This could be wrong. It's possible institutions adapt faster than I'm giving them credit for, or that some threshold of model capability makes the trust gap collapse. But the 42% and rising discontinuation rate suggests we're not there yet.</span> Every playbook written to onboard employee #50 is a Mosaic feature in disguise. Every decision framework that lets a junior FDE act without calling Hirsh is actual IP. If the company treats scaling as overhead rather than product development, the most important IP gets thrown away. <span class="highlight-text">That conversion capability is the product.</span>

## What I'd be watching

- Whether the research lab connects to the deployment business or stays academic. Percepta's specific advantage over Distyl and Brain Co is the in-house research lab doing model-level work plus embedded teams generating curated domain data the labs can't access. If those two connect, the lab trains on data the deployments generate, the deployments use models the lab produces. That's a flywheel nobody else can build. <span class="marginnote">There's growing evidence that models trained on curated, credentialed sources outperform general models in domains like healthcare. If that holds, the highest-value research isn't general architecture (competing with Anthropic) but specialized models from curated clinical data.</span>

- <span class="marginnote">Without deployment data, the research is academic. Without proprietary research, the deployment is just consulting. I don't know what the combination becomes but it's clearly different from either one alone. If the lab's published work has no production connection to deployments after six months, it's window dressing.</span> Whether Mosaic is actually learning or just accumulating the feeling of learning. Playbooks get written, dashboards fill up. Does deployment six take less time than deployment one because of something in Mosaic, or because the team got experienced and the playbooks are just a record of that. <span class="marginnote">If the experienced people leave, what does Mosaic actually contain?</span>

- The self-obsolescence problem. Successful deployments train the client to not need you. The better the team, the faster knowledge transfers. <span class="marginnote">Palantir had Foundry creating platform lock-in. Percepta builds on Claude. The client can hire their own engineers and point them at Claude. Each engagement has a ceiling unless Mosaic accumulates institutional intelligence faster than the client absorbs it.</span>

- Whether the structural thesis is right about the world but wrong about who captures the value. Deployment complexity persists. That doesn't mean a startup owned by a holding company is the entity that captures it. <span class="marginnote">I keep wanting to collapse "thesis is right" into "Percepta wins" and I don't trust how good that feels.</span>

- The uncomfortable version of the GC relationship: Percepta does the work, GC's assets get smarter, GC captures most of the upside. <span class="marginnote">I don't know how you think about that from inside.</span>

## What would change my mind

- Deployment six same resources as deployment one. Compounding isn't happening.

- Headcount linear with revenue. It's consulting.

- Research has no production connection to deployments in six months. Decorative.

- A near-term model walks a generalist through compliant healthcare deployment end-to-end. Quality gap closed.

- The four GC pieces (capital, assets, deployment, research) don't connect as a system. It's a holding company with a consulting subsidiary.

- I don't know whether Percepta is unforkable today. That bothers me more than anything on this list. But I think the trajectory is right and the window is open and the team is moving fast enough that the answer could be different in a year.
